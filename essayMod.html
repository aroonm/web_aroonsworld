Big Data in the Game Industry

International Journal of Applied Engineering Research (IJAER)

The amount of available data has exploded over the past few years as a result of social behavior and social trends and due to the rapid growth in technology and software systems. Big Data has become a key driver for innovation and growth especially in sectors that rely on technologies like Cloud computing, Internet of things and data analytics.

Even a couple of decades ago, data was scarce. This has changed in such a short period of time. The main reason being the growth of the internet. The internet is a massive warehouse of data. Practically everything on the internet is recorded. When you search for something on Google, your query and your subsequent clicks are recorded. When you purchase an item off Amazon or eBay, not only is your purchase recorded but also your every action before and after the purchase. Every link you click and every image you open is recorded and logged. All this data forms a digital data footprint that you leave behind. All this data can be integrated with demographics, advertising and social media activity. This new type of data is much larger, and has a much greater scope as it includes new and unique observations and measurements.

The game industry is one which will benefit on tremendous scales from Big Data. This is largely due to the fact that games produce large volumes of data, from every interactive step any user takes in the game, right down to joystick movements. This data can in turn deliver fascinating solutions for enhancing gamer experience, tailoring games for particular users and eventually increasing revenues, because the goal of every game is to produce in their gamers a feeling of addictiveness that will make the gamers come back again and again. Social games, that are available nowadays on social media at the most minimal of prices, probably generate the largest volumes of data, and thus exploit Big Data to the fullest.

During the course of this paper, the basic concepts regarding Big Data are reviewed, followed by a detailed description of Big Data as it is used in the game industry. A survey of social gaming giants, Zynga was conducted to buttress the contents of the paper.

Keywords: Big Data, Analytics, Mobile, Social Games, Survey

Prof. Senthil Jayavel and Aroon Philip Mathai

Social Physics and Big Data

This paper presents an overview of how big data is involved in the field of social physics. It also elucidates how social physics can derive inferences and patterns regarding the behavior of individuals or the society that they reside in, from unstructured or irrelevant data. In order to provide more insights into the role of social physics in an individual's life, specific details about the way social physics affects various aspects of life like healthcare, security, economic predictions, political polarization, etc. has been explained. Then we talk about how idea flow is the main aspect of social physics, supported with real life scenarios and mathematical equations. Also this paper offers an explanation on how big data is a tool for social physics and how related both are. The concluding remarks will summarize the paper.

Social physics is a new data science that elucidates how a bulk of people behave and hence creates executable/implementable intelligence giving us more information about how they would retaliate or act to a specific circumstance and allows us to predict their behavior.

It demands social, analytical, software and socio-management sciences to form an approach towards synthesizing a more resilient and creation-oriented society while at the same time providing higher security, personnel privacy and resistance to cyber-attacks. Social physics has stirred our lives in quite some fields, for example, by bringing improvement in the health care department, eliminating gridlocks worldwide, stabilizing financial markets and stock exchanges. This has significantly increased our personal privacy and digital safety and security, potentially allowing us to unlock the intelligence and prudence of the common public in a way that can foster an efficient government.

Keywords: Social Physics, Big Data, Interaction, Behavior, Creative Society, Social Networks

Creativity, Inc.

Managers tend to choose safe ways to stay profitable rather than coming up with creative and innovative solutions to current and future problems. In Creativity, Inc. Ed Catmull offers insight through a series of anecdotes of his time at Pixar and Disney on how to avoid such creativity-stifling pitfalls. The book is also an opportunity to see Pixar stories from much closer than ever before. Here are some of the top insights that resonated with me;

  • People are More Important than Ideas
    Many people believe that success in a business is the result of a groundbreaking idea. Although this might not be wrong, Ed Catmull suggests that a more crucial component is hiring the right people. "Give a good idea to a mediocre team, and they will screw it up. But give a mediocre idea to a great team, and they will either fix it or come up with something better." Think of the iPhone. The iPhone is more the cumulative effort of several people coming together and sharing their insights to create a wonderful product, rather than just the evolution of an idea. However fantastic your idea maybe, putting together the right team to execute and develop this idea might have a greater impact.
  • Encourage Candor
    Creativity will flourish when you eliminate impediments such as uncertainty, instability, hidden problems and lack of candor. People try to avoid failure because innately failure is embarrassing and it hurts. But failure isn’t necessarily a bad thing. In fact Ed Catmull encourages people to fail early and to fail fast because ahead of failure lies success.
  • Organizational Hierarchy
    Listen to what everyone has to say. Everybody should be able to talk to everybody. But unlike its communication structure a company’s organizational structure should be autocratic and not democratic. Executive control and a team lead is crucial.
  • Do Things Differently
    Although rules can be comforting, they can stifle innovation. People don’t get to decide if they’re doing something better but they can do things differently. This might lead to a lot of unprecedented outcomes, but embrace them and more importantly, learn from them.
  • Making Mistakes
    People tend to compromise on innovation to avoid mistakes. However more often than not, the cost of preventing errors is far greater than the cost of fixing them.

Change is inevitable and invaluable for creative environments. A company’s culture can only be truly creative when focus is placed on putting together a great team, building trust and fostering an imaginative, free environment.

Zero to One: Notes on Start Ups, or How to Build the Future

"Doing what we already know how to do takes the world from 1 to n, adding more of something familiar. But every time we create something new, we go from 0 to 1. The act of creation is singular, as is the moment of creation, and the result is something fresh and strange."

  • Following Past Trends
    The narrator suggests that the future will not be defined by Globalization but rather by Technology. He exemplifies this point by bringing up China and the Chinese air pollution problem. With limited resources Globalization is unsustainable.
  • Avoid Competition
    While US airlines serve millions of passengers and create hundreds of billions of dollars of value every year, they make only 37 cents per passenger per trip. On the other hand, Google has 100 times higher profit margin than the entire airline industry.
    Monopolies are bad only in a world without change. Microsoft’s monopoly in operating systems has been overthrown by Apple’s iOS and Google’s Android.
  • Build a Monopoly
    The narrator suggests to start small and monopolize. It doesn’t make sense to get 1% of a $100 billion market. Such a market lacks a good starting point and is always open to competition and reaching that 1% will be difficult. If at all that 1% is reached, the intense competition will stifle profits. It makes more sense to find and dominate a niche market and gradually expand to broader markets.
  • Power Law
    If you look at Founders Fund’s 2005 fund, the best investment ended up being worth about as much as all the rest combined. And the investment in the second best company was about as valuable as number three through the rest. This is the power law distribution in practice.
  • Foundations of the Team
    The author encourages a founder to know the co-founder before the business. The board of directors should not exceed five people and three is ideal. All employees should be full-time. No part-time employees, contractors and vendors.
  • Sales
    The importance of sales should not be underestimated. Both sales and the product are important.
